Chapter 8: Resolving Conflicts

Resolving Conflicts

Introduction

Conflict is a disagreement between people who perceive that their goals, needs, or interests are incompatible. It is not always bad. When handled well, it can clarify problems and improve understanding. When handled poorly, it can damage trust and create resentment. Rose (2025) explains that conflict often involves more than the surface issue because people are also reacting to tone, perception, and emotion.

It is important to be able to resolve conflicts because unresolved conflict rarely disappears. It usually sits in the background and shows up again later, sometimes more intense than before. Rose (2025) describes this as a latency period, where conflict may look settled on the outside but is still waiting underneath. People who can resolve conflict are more likely to keep relationships functional and avoid turning one disagreement into a long pattern of frustration.

One strategy for resolving conflict is the R.E.S.O.L.V.E technique: Recognize the conflict, Engage with empathy, Separate people from the problem, Open up to solutions, Lead with clear communication, Validate and follow up, and Establish preventative measures. In short, that means identifying the real issue, listening to the other person, focusing on the problem instead of attacking the person, working toward a solution, communicating clearly, checking back later, and learning from the experience so it does not keep repeating. This connects to what we have covered in class because it combines listening, empathy, and respectful communication, which Rose (2025) identifies as key tools for healthy conflict resolution.

A Conflict I Experienced

About five years ago, during my last quarter before earning my AA degree, I had a conflict with my software development instructor over a final assignment. All quarter we had been building a simple fake ecommerce website for a culinary business selling things like barbecue sauce, seasonings, and cooking equipment. His earlier assignments had taken me only minutes to finish because I was already a fairly experienced programmer and the class was easy for me. For the final, he wanted documentation for the site. I turned in a couple of pages describing the pages, buttons, and a few user flows for purchasing an item. I thought that matched the scope of the project. He gave me an F, which meant I would fail the class right before graduating.

When I met with him the first time, I learned he had been expecting something much longer, around twenty pages, even though the assignment instructions did not say that. I asked if I could redo it, and he said no. I was upset, and fairness mattered a lot to me because the expectations had not been clear. I used SCC's formal process for complaining about an instructor and met with the dean. They sympathized with me, but they sided with the instructor. I was a younger and edgier person back then, and looking back, I may not have spoken in the most respectful tone during that process. That is worth acknowledging. I was frustrated and felt like I was being treated unfairly, but my delivery probably did not help me in the moment.

The conflict did eventually get resolved in a later meeting with my instructor. Even though the dean did not force him to do anything, he chose to let me redo the assignment and gave me full credit. In that same meeting, he looked at me and said, "You want a job?" He told me he was impressed by my professionalism and determination. I was relieved because I did not want a failed class on my record, and I was surprised that the conversation turned around that fast. I said yes, redid the documentation, passed, graduated, and I still work with him today. I appreciate him a lot. But most of the conflict before that point could have been handled better. Looking back, he probably would have let me redo it without much trouble if I had turned it in early and he had graded it before the due date, but I turned it in the night it was due. That timing made everything harder.

Using R.E.S.O.L.V.E, the real issue was not just the grade. It was unclear expectations about what "good documentation" meant. I should have Recognized that sooner and asked more questions before turning anything in. After I got the F, I needed to Engage with empathy and Separate the person from the problem, meaning focus on the missing standards instead of treating him like the enemy. I could have used clearer I-statements in that first meeting, like "I feel blindsided by the F because I did not see length requirements in the instructions. I need to understand what you were looking for" (Rose, 2025). Opening up to solutions could have meant asking for a rubric or example earlier instead of assuming my short documentation was enough just because the site itself was simple. With the dean, I needed calmer communication too. The situation did end with follow-up and a real solution, but it took more stress than it needed to because the expectations and tone were not handled well until that final meeting with my instructor.

Reflection

Self-Awareness Questions

When I am in a conflict, anxiety is usually the first thing I notice. It shows up as tension in my chest and shoulders, and my brain starts running through possible outcomes before I say anything. If the conflict involves criticism or something that feels unfair, I also feel it in my stomach, like a drop or tightness. Anger is usually not my first outward reaction, but I feel it internally as heat in my face and a strong urge to shut down or become very logical. During the documentation conflict, I remember feeling that stomach-drop feeling when I saw the F, and then a lot of mental rehearsing before I talked to my instructor and the dean. I was not yelling, but I was definitely wound up.

Adaptability Questions

I cannot use the same approach in every conflict. With an instructor or authority figure, I need to stay respectful even when I think I am right, because tone can matter as much as the argument. In a family disagreement, I may need more patience and privacy. In a political disagreement, I may need to decide whether the conversation is even worth having. The documentation situation taught me that persistence can help, but so can asking better questions before a problem gets that big. I also need to calm down first when I am stressed (Carver et al., 1989), but then come back to the conversation instead of using that as an excuse to disappear or blow up.

Impact Questions

To de-escalate conflict, I would use listening, empathy, and better timing. Rose (2025) emphasizes that listening is one of the best tools because people often escalate when they feel unheard. I would also choose a private setting instead of a public one, use I-statements, and focus on the problem instead of attacking the person. In the documentation conflict, I did eventually get a good outcome in that final meeting with my instructor, but I do not think my early approach fully de-escalated things. Going to the dean added pressure, and my tone during that process probably made the situation harder. A calmer approach earlier, especially before I escalated, might have solved it with less damage. A better version of my approach would be calm plus clear.

Empathy Questions

To show empathy, I need to understand why the conflict matters to the other person even if I disagree with them. That means asking questions, listening without interrupting, and acknowledging their frustration before I explain my own view. With my instructor, empathy would have meant trying to understand his standard for documentation instead of only seeing the F as unfair. Maybe he really did want thorough professional documentation and thought the assignment implied that, even if he did not spell it out clearly. With the dean, empathy would have meant understanding that they often have to support instructors while still hearing student concerns. Compassion is one of my core values, and it should show up in conflict too, even when I feel wronged.

Feedback and Improvement Questions

I have already gotten useful feedback from my mother about being too passive in conflict, but this experience was the opposite problem in some ways. I advocated for myself, which I am glad I did, but I probably came across sharper than I needed to at the time, especially with the dean. To improve, I could ask for feedback more directly after disagreements, like "Did I explain my concern clearly?" or "Did my tone make this harder to resolve?" I could also ask for standards earlier, such as "What does a strong submission look like for this assignment?" That probably would have helped more than waiting until after the F. My instructor's response in that final meeting was a kind of feedback too. He saw determination in how I handled the situation, even if I was not perfect along the way.

Cultural Sensitivity Questions

My normal approach is not universal. I tend to value privacy, politeness, and avoiding public embarrassment, but Rose (2025) explains that communication and conflict expectations vary across cultures. In an academic or workplace setting, some people expect very direct advocacy when something feels unfair, while others expect more restraint and private problem-solving first. Someone used to strict hierarchy may see my appeal to the dean as appropriate, while someone else may see it as escalating too fast. I need to pay attention to the setting and the other person's expectations instead of assuming my version of fairness is the only valid one.

Overall, resolving conflict is not the same as avoiding it politely, but it is also not the same as fighting until someone wins. I learned that from this experience. I was right to care about fairness and to keep going, but I could have communicated better earlier and taken more responsibility for my tone. The best outcome was not just getting the redo. It was that one meeting with my instructor turned the conflict into a job and a working relationship I still have today.
