Chapter 4: Family & Culture

Family, Culture, and Conflict

Introduction

Family and culture have a major effect on how people deal with conflict because they shape what conflict even feels like in the first place. Family is usually where people first learn whether disagreement is normal, disrespectful, private, loud, avoidable, or something that has to be solved right away. Culture adds another layer by teaching people what counts as polite, what counts as honest, and whether the priority should be individual expression or keeping peace with the group.

One way to understand this is through family systems theory. Bowen (1978) argued that people are shaped by the emotional patterns in their families, not just by individual personality. That matters for conflict because people often carry family habits into later relationships. If someone grows up around yelling, they may see yelling as normal or become very sensitive to it. If someone grows up around avoidance, they may learn to stay quiet even when something bothers them. Communication research also shows that people bring different conflict styles and expectations into disagreements, which affects whether they avoid, compete, compromise, or cooperate (Rose, 2025).

Culture also affects conflict because it shapes values. Rose (2025) explains that individualistic cultures tend to place more emphasis on personal needs and goals, while collectivistic cultures tend to place more emphasis on group harmony. This difference can change how people approach conflict. For example, a person from a more individualistic background may think it is healthy to state their opinion directly. A person from a background that values harmony may think direct confrontation is rude or unnecessary. Neither person is automatically wrong, but the mismatch can create conflict by itself.

Overall, family and culture influence whether someone avoids conflict, confronts it directly, tries to smooth it over, or wants a private conversation. For example, if someone comes from a family where conflict is handled privately, they may feel embarrassed or disrespected if another person calls them out in public. In that situation, the conflict is not only about the original issue. It is also about different expectations for how conflict should be handled.

Family Background and Culture

My family background has affected my approach to conflict a lot. The main people in my family makeup for this chapter are my mother, my father, and my older sister. My relationship with my older sister probably shaped me the most. I was closest to her growing up, but we also argued all the time. Because of that, conflict felt like a normal part of a close relationship instead of proof that the relationship was falling apart.

Conflict in my family was usually supposed to be handled through some kind of calm discussion, even if it sometimes became less calm. I learned that if someone is hurting you or doing something that really bothers you, the best response is usually a proper private conversation, not blowing up in public. At the same time, each person in my family handled conflict differently. My father was bad about making up after a fight. He might stop talking for a few days and then eventually act like nothing happened. My mother could sometimes be that way too, but she usually calmed down faster and was less likely to completely pretend the conflict never happened. My sister and I were different because we had so much experience arguing that we usually made up very quickly, sometimes only moments later.

I think these family patterns still affect me. I usually prefer to keep conflict contained and private. I do not like public scenes, and I do not respect people who embarrass someone instead of dealing with the actual issue. That connects to communication research because conflict styles and expectations can shape what a person sees as a normal or mature response to disagreement (Rose, 2025). My family's pattern taught me to value calmness, but it may have also taught me to be too passive sometimes.

My culture is harder to summarize in the usual way because it is not mainly about race, ethnicity, gender expectations, or strict family roles. My culture is more like a mix of interests, habits, and values. It is classic rock and indie pop, The Lord of the Rings, Star Wars, D&D, Minecraft, exercise, sugar-free ice cream, sushi, fried potatoes, AI, saving money, and living with your parents. It is also individualism, probably to a fault. I generally value politeness, not bullying people, and not getting involved in pointless drama.

That still counts as culture because culture includes the meanings, communities, and values people live with. My culture has shaped my conflict management by making me more likely to stay polite, avoid humiliating people, and stay out of conflict unless there is a real reason to get involved. Rose's (2025) explanation of individualism fits here because I often assume people should mostly mind their own business unless they are actually helping. That can be useful because it keeps me from escalating every disagreement. It can also be a weakness because sometimes not getting involved is just avoidance.

Reflection

Self-Awareness Questions

My family and culture influence my perception of conflict by making me pay attention to privacy, tone, and whether the conflict is being handled respectfully. I usually think conflict should be direct but not public. For example, if someone has a problem with me, I would much rather they talk to me privately than embarrass me in front of other people. If they make it public, I may focus less on their point and more on the fact that they handled it badly.

Adaptability Questions

Someone from my family and culture can adapt by staying polite without becoming passive. One strategy is to ask for a private conversation while still being direct about the issue. Another strategy is to recognize when someone else needs more emotional expression than I do. For example, if someone is upset and wants to talk through their feelings first, I should not treat that as unnecessary drama. I can listen first and then move toward solving the problem.

Impact Questions

My family and culture can de-escalate conflict because I usually try not to insult people, create scenes, or make disagreement bigger than it needs to be. I value staying calm and not bullying anyone. However, these same habits can also escalate conflict in a quieter way if I avoid speaking up. If I wait too long to say what bothers me, the problem can build up until I am more frustrated than I needed to be.

Empathy Questions

Someone from my family and culture could show empathy by respecting the other person's dignity during conflict. To me, empathy means listening, not interrupting, and not trying to humiliate the other person just because I disagree with them. It also means understanding that people from different families may react differently. Someone who gets loud may not be trying to be cruel. Someone who gets quiet may not be trying to be dismissive.

Feedback and Improvement Questions

I have received feedback from my mother that I can be too passive or submissive in conflict. I understand why she says that, because I sometimes stay calm by holding back too much. What I learned is that being calm is not always the same as handling conflict well. If I were giving advice to someone from a similar background, I would say that politeness is good, but it cannot replace boundaries. If something matters, it is better to say it clearly before resentment builds.

Cultural Sensitivity Questions

To resolve conflict with people from different family and cultural backgrounds, I need to remember that my normal approach is not universal. Some people are used to loud arguments that end quickly. Some people come from families where conflict is avoided completely. Some people need direct honesty, while others need a softer approach. I can handle those differences better by asking questions, paying attention to the other person's comfort level, and not assuming that my private and restrained style is automatically the best one.

Overall, my family and culture have taught me to value private discussion, politeness, and restraint in conflict. Those lessons help me avoid unnecessary drama, but they can also make me too passive. The main thing I take from this is that conflict habits are learned, which means they can also be adjusted. I do not need to become aggressive, but I do need to be direct when something actually matters.
