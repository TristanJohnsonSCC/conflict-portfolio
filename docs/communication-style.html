Chapter 5: Communication Style

Communication Style and Conflict

Introduction

Communication style is the general way a person expresses thoughts, emotions, and needs when dealing with other people. In conflict, it matters because people respond not just to the issue, but also to tone, timing, directness, and whether they feel respected or dismissed (Rose, 2025).

Communication styles are often grouped into four broad categories: assertive, passive, passive-aggressive, and aggressive. Assertive communication is direct and respectful. Passive communication involves holding back or prioritizing peace over self-expression. Passive-aggressive communication is indirect and often shows up through sarcasm, resentment, or mixed messages. Aggressive communication is the most confrontational and usually focuses more on control than resolution (Alberti & Emmons, 2017; Rose, 2025).

These styles shape how conflict develops. Assertive communication tends to support clarity and problem-solving, while passive communication can delay issues until frustration builds. Passive-aggressive communication can make conflict confusing because the real problem is not stated openly, and aggressive communication can turn disagreement into a power struggle. Because of that, communication style strongly affects whether conflict gets resolved, avoided, or made worse.

Communication style also affects how people interpret each other. A person may believe they are being calm, honest, or efficient while the other person experiences them as cold, unclear, or disrespectful. In that sense, conflict is partly about what is said and partly about how it is delivered. Understanding communication styles does not solve every disagreement, but it helps explain why the same conflict can feel very different to the people involved.

My Communication Style

My communication style is mostly passive. In conflict, my instinct is usually to hold back, stay calm, and avoid making the situation bigger than it needs to be. I prefer private conversations over public confrontation, and I would usually rather keep the peace than create more tension.

Passive communication is generally indirect and conflict-avoidant. People with this style may struggle to set boundaries, apologize too quickly, or act agreeable even when they are frustrated. In the short term, that can keep things smooth, but in the long term it can create resentment because the real issue never gets addressed clearly (Alberti & Emmons, 2017). That description feels mostly accurate to me.

This style fits because I value restraint. If I am upset, my first instinct is to analyze the situation and decide whether speaking up is worth it. That can be useful because it keeps me from reacting impulsively, but it also means I sometimes stay quiet too long. What looks like maturity can really be hesitation, and by the time I speak, I may already be more frustrated than I should be.

I also think this style fits because I pay close attention to whether a conflict seems productive. If a disagreement feels pointless, overly emotional, or likely to turn into a scene, my instinct is usually to pull back rather than push harder. That protects me from unnecessary drama, but it can also make me too cautious. Sometimes a conversation needs a direct answer even if it is uncomfortable.

I do not think being passive means being weak, and I do not think becoming aggressive would improve my communication. A lot of my passivity comes from wanting to stay respectful and avoid humiliating people. Still, I can see the downside. Passive communication may de-escalate a disagreement at first, but it can also delay honesty and resolution. Research on assertiveness suggests that the most effective communication is usually direct without being hostile, which is the balance I need more of (Ames, 2008; Alberti & Emmons, 2017).

So for me, the goal is not to become a completely different kind of person. It is to keep the strengths of my current style, like restraint and thoughtfulness, while becoming clearer about what I actually think and need. If I can do that, then my communication would be less passive and more assertive without becoming harsh.

Reflection

Self-Awareness Questions

My communication style makes me focus first on tone and respect. I usually see conflict as something that should be handled privately and calmly, not dramatically or in public. For example, if someone criticized me in front of a group, I would probably react more negatively to the public embarrassment than to the actual criticism. If the same issue were brought up privately, I would respond much better.

That also means I sometimes judge the way a conflict is handled before I judge the issue itself. If the delivery feels disrespectful, it becomes harder for me to stay focused on the original point. That is something I need to be aware of, because even poorly delivered criticism can still contain something I should hear.

Adaptability Questions

Someone with a passive communication style can adapt by learning to be direct earlier instead of waiting until frustration builds. Useful strategies are calm, clear statements and reminding yourself that speaking up is not the same as creating drama. If I were in conflict with another passive person, one of us would need to bring up the real issue instead of pretending everything was fine. If I were dealing with someone aggressive, I would need to stay calm while still holding my ground. For example, if a classmate kept interrupting me, I could say, "I want to finish my point before we move on," instead of saying nothing.

Another useful strategy would be setting small goals for difficult conversations. Instead of trying to say everything perfectly, I could focus on clearly stating one concern and one boundary. That would make conflict feel more manageable and would help me practice being direct without feeling like I have to become confrontational.

Impact Questions

My communication style can contribute to both de-escalation and escalation. It helps de-escalate conflict because I usually stay calm and avoid making the situation harsher than it needs to be. But it can also escalate conflict in a delayed way. If I avoid saying what is wrong, the other person may not know there is a problem, and my frustration can build in the background.

That delayed frustration is probably the biggest risk in my style. A conflict can look resolved on the surface even when it is not. If that keeps happening, the next disagreement may feel bigger than it really is because it is carrying leftovers from older issues that were never fully addressed.

Empathy Questions

Someone with my communication style can show empathy by listening carefully, staying calm, and giving the other person room to explain their perspective. In conflict, that means acknowledging their frustration and showing that disagreement is not the same as disrespect. At the same time, empathy should not erase my own point of view. I still need to be honest about what I need.

I think this matters because passive people can sometimes confuse empathy with giving in. Real empathy is not just agreeing to keep the peace. It is understanding the other person while still participating honestly in the conversation.

Feedback and Improvement Questions

I have received feedback from my mother that I can be too passive and submissive in conflict. I understand why she says that because I do tend to hold back when things are tense. What I learned is that there is a difference between being calm and being clear. If I were giving advice to other people with a passive communication style, I would say that politeness matters, but if something is important, it still needs to be said clearly.

I think that is the biggest improvement point for me. I do not need to be louder, harsher, or more dramatic. I need to say important things sooner so they do not turn into private frustration.

Cultural Sensitivity Questions

These communication styles do not apply the same way across every culture. In some cultures, directness is seen as confidence and honesty. In others, it may sound rude or disrespectful. Because of that, communication styles are better understood as broad patterns than rigid labels, and I should not assume that my preference for restraint and private discussion is universal (Rose, 2025).

That is important in conflict because people can misread each other very easily across different backgrounds. One person may think they are being mature by staying quiet, while another may see that same behavior as evasive. Another person may think they are being straightforward, while someone else experiences them as aggressive. Cultural awareness helps keep those differences from becoming a second conflict on top of the first one.

Overall, this chapter makes it clear to me that my communication style has both strengths and weaknesses. My passivity helps me stay thoughtful and respectful, but it also keeps me from being as clear and assertive as I should be. If I want to handle conflict better, I need to stay calm without disappearing.
