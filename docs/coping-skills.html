Chapter 7: Coping Skills

Coping Skills and Conflict

Introduction

Coping skills are the strategies people use to manage stress, anxiety, and difficult emotions. They can include problem-solving, calming down, distraction, seeking support, or changing the way someone thinks about a situation. Carver, Scheier, and Weintraub (1989) describe coping as a set of different responses people use when they are under stress, including problem-focused strategies, emotion-focused strategies, and less helpful strategies. That matters because coping does not just affect how someone feels. It also affects how clearly they think and communicate.

Coping skills relate directly to conflict because conflict usually brings emotion with it. Even when a disagreement starts with a practical issue, stress can change how people hear each other. A calm comment may sound like criticism, or a delay may feel like disrespect. Rose (2025) explains that communication is shaped by context, perception, and emotion, not just by the words themselves. In conflict, people are often reacting to what they think the situation means, not only to what was literally said.

Stress, anxiety, and negative emotions can make conflict harder to manage effectively. When someone is overwhelmed, they may become defensive, impatient, passive, or focused on protecting themselves. They may stop listening carefully and start assuming bad intent. For someone who already tends to be passive in conflict, stress can make it easier to stay quiet too long. For someone more aggressive, stress can make it easier to attack instead of explain.

Coping skills can help because they create space between the feeling and the response. A person who can calm down, rethink the situation, or step back before reacting is more likely to communicate clearly. That does not mean coping skills make conflict easy. It means they can keep the conflict from being controlled completely by stress. Overall, coping skills are part of communication because they affect the kind of communicator a person becomes under pressure.

How I Cope with Stress, Anxiety, and Negative Emotions

I do find it challenging to manage stress, anxiety, and difficult emotions sometimes, even though I usually try to stay calm on the outside. My instinct is often to pull back and think before I say much. That can be helpful because it keeps me from reacting impulsively, but it can also become a weakness if I stay quiet too long. A lot of my conflict pattern is not loud escalation. It is more like stress building privately while I decide whether the issue is worth bringing up.

One coping strategy that helps me is using low-stakes activities to decompress. Music, games, and other comfort activities give me a way to get out of my own head for a while. They do not directly solve the problem, but they lower the intensity enough that I am less likely to respond from frustration. Carver et al. (1989) describe emotion-focused coping as strategies that manage the emotional response to stress rather than immediately changing the situation itself. That fits this kind of coping.

This benefits my ability to resolve conflict because it gives me time to cool down. If I try to have a difficult conversation while I am already stressed, I am more likely to be passive, impatient, or mentally checked out. If I decompress first, I am more likely to speak in a calm and respectful way. That connects to my communication style because I already value private, contained conversations over public conflict.

At the same time, this strategy has limits. If I use distraction only to avoid the issue, then it stops being helpful. Playing a game or listening to music can help me calm down, but it cannot replace actually saying what needs to be said. One coping strategy I would like to try is cognitive reappraisal, which means asking whether there is another way to interpret the situation before reacting. For me, that would mean asking, "What else could this mean?" or "Am I reacting to the facts, or to the story I am telling myself?" Since Rose (2025) emphasizes how much communication depends on perception, reappraisal could help me check my perception before I communicate from it.

Reflection

Self-Awareness Questions

When I am stressed, anxious, or filled with negative emotions, I become more focused on tone, respect, and whether the conversation seems productive. If I already feel overwhelmed, I may interpret disagreement as pointless or emotionally messy before I really give it a chance. I also become more likely to pull back and analyze instead of participating directly.

One example is when I am around people I strongly disagree with politically. My normal reaction is not to jump into a dramatic argument. I usually go quiet and start analyzing what they are saying, what assumptions they are making, and whether the conversation is even worth having. That can prevent unnecessary escalation, but it can also make me too passive. If I am stressed, I may mentally check out instead of trying to communicate in a useful way.

Adaptability Questions

When I am dealing with difficult emotions, I do find it harder to adapt in conflict situations. My normal strengths are staying calm, thinking before I speak, and trying not to make the conflict bigger than it needs to be. But when I am stressed, those strengths can become too rigid. Staying calm can turn into shutting down. Thinking before speaking can turn into overthinking. Avoiding drama can turn into avoiding the conversation completely.

If a disagreement feels emotional or chaotic, my instinct is usually to back away from it instead of adjusting to what the other person needs. Someone else may need to talk through their feelings first, while I may want to skip straight to whether the conversation is logical or productive. A better response would be to stay calm while still being willing to engage, listen, and ask questions.

Impact Questions

My coping skills can contribute to both de-escalating and escalating conflict. They help de-escalate conflict when they give me time to calm down before I respond. Music, games, and stepping away from the intensity for a while can stop me from reacting too quickly. Since I do not usually want to create a scene or humiliate anyone, my coping style often keeps conflict quieter and more controlled.

However, my coping can also escalate conflict in a delayed way. If I cope by distracting myself or pulling back but never return to the issue, then the problem does not actually get solved. It just sits there. That can lead to resentment or frustration later. The conflict may look calm on the outside, but it is not really resolved.

Empathy Questions

When I am experiencing high stress, I do find it harder to be empathetic in conflict situations. I do not think I stop caring about the other person, but I become more focused on getting relief from the stress. I may care more about ending the conversation, making it logical, or getting back to normal than about fully understanding how the other person feels.

This is especially true when someone is emotional in a way that feels inefficient or dramatic to me. My instinct may be to analyze the problem instead of sitting with the emotion. But conflict is still happening between people, not just arguments. If I want to handle conflict better, I need to remember that empathy means showing I understand the other person's perspective before trying to fix or correct anything.

Feedback and Improvement Questions

I have received feedback from my mother that I can be too passive or submissive in conflict. I understand why she says that, because I do tend to hold back when things are tense. I do not think becoming aggressive would be better, but I do think there is a difference between being calm and being unclear. If my coping strategy is always to stay quiet until I feel less stressed, then other people may not know what I actually think or need.

If I were giving advice to someone who feels overwhelmed in conflict, I would tell them to calm down first, but not disappear. Taking time to regulate is useful, but it should eventually lead back to communication. I would also tell them to separate the facts from the story they are telling themselves and to say the important thing earlier. It does not have to be harsh or dramatic, but it does need to be clear.

Cultural Sensitivity Questions

These coping skills would not apply to every person or culture in exactly the same way. Some people and cultures value direct emotional expression more than I do. Others may value restraint, privacy, or group harmony more strongly. Because of that, coping strategies should not be treated like one-size-fits-all rules. A strategy that helps me calm down, like stepping away from the intensity for a while, might make someone else feel ignored or rejected.

Coping also depends on what people have access to and what their culture sees as healthy. Music, games, and private decompression may work for me, but other people may cope better through family support, religious practices, community, direct conversation, or physical activity. Rose (2025) explains that communication is shaped by cultural expectations, and coping is similar. The goal is to manage stress in a way that still supports respectful communication and does not make conflict worse.

Overall, coping skills are closely connected to conflict. When I manage stress well, I am more likely to communicate clearly and stay respectful. When I cope by avoiding, distracting myself too long, or overthinking, conflict can stay unresolved even if it looks calm. The main thing I take from this is that I need to keep the coping strategies that help me calm down while also becoming more direct after I have had time to think.
