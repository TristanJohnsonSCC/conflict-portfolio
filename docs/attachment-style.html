Chapter 2: Attachment Style

Introduction Page: Attachment Theory and Conflict

Attachment theory is basically a way of explaining how people bond with others and how they respond to closeness, distance, support, and conflict in relationships. John Bowlby first developed the theory and argued that early relationships with caregivers help shape a person’s expectations about trust, safety, and connection (Bowlby, 1969). Mary Ainsworth later built on that work by identifying different attachment patterns in children, and those ideas are now often applied to adult relationships too (Ainsworth et al., 1978).

Attachment theory is usually broken into four main styles: secure, anxious, avoidant, and disorganized. Secure attachment usually means someone is comfortable with closeness and trust. Anxious attachment is more connected to needing reassurance, being sensitive to rejection, and worrying about abandonment. Avoidant attachment is often associated with emotional distance and a tendency to pull away when things get too intense. Disorganized attachment is more mixed and can involve wanting closeness while also fearing it. These are broad patterns, not perfect labels, but they are useful for understanding relationship behavior.

Attachment theory connects pretty directly to conflict because conflict is not just about the issue itself. It also involves trust, emotional safety, and how people read each other’s behavior. Someone with a secure attachment style may be more likely to stay calm and communicate clearly. Someone with an anxious attachment style may read distance or criticism as rejection, which can make conflict feel bigger than it is. Someone with an avoidant style may shut down or pull away, while someone with a disorganized style may react in inconsistent ways.

Since conflict can trigger fears of rejection, abandonment, or being misunderstood, attachment style can shape how someone reacts. A person who is worried about losing connection may become passive, overly apologetic, or focused on fixing everything right away. A person who protects themselves through distance may avoid hard conversations altogether. So even though attachment style does not explain everything, it is a helpful way to understand why people handle conflict so differently (Mikulincer & Shaver, 2016).

Research Page: Anxious Attachment Style

My attachment style is anxious attachment. In general, this style is marked by wanting closeness and connection, but also worrying about rejection, distance, or abandonment. People with this style often care a lot about their relationships, but they can also become very sensitive to signs that someone is upset, pulling away, or losing interest. In adult relationships, anxious attachment has been linked to reassurance-seeking, emotional intensity, and overthinking communication when something feels off (Hazan & Shaver, 1987; Mikulincer & Shaver, 2016).

Anxious attachment can make someone really attentive to relationships, but that can also turn into stress. Instead of assuming a disagreement is manageable, a person with this style may worry that the relationship itself is at risk. That can lead to overthinking, people-pleasing, apologizing too fast, or becoming passive just to keep things from getting worse. Sometimes it can also lead to needing reassurance before feeling okay again.

This description feels mostly accurate to me. I care a lot about keeping relationships stable, and I would usually rather keep the peace than add more tension. I can definitely see how that can make me seem passive or submissive in conflict, especially if I think being more direct will just create more drama or hurt feelings. At the same time, I do not think anxious attachment automatically means someone is weak. In my case, I think it has also made me more aware of other people’s feelings and more careful about how I respond.

One reason this style fits is that I sometimes focus more on keeping the relationship okay than on fully saying what I am frustrated about. If I can tell someone is upset, I usually want to calm things down first instead of pushing my own point right away. That can make me hold back too much sometimes, but it has also helped me get along with a lot of different people and avoid turning small issues into bigger ones.

Overall, anxious attachment explains both my strengths and my challenges in conflict. It can make me empathetic and motivated to protect relationships, but it can also make it harder to stay confident in my own point of view when I think disagreement might damage the connection. So for me, the goal is not to care less. It is to care while also being more direct.

Reflection Page: Anxious Attachment Style and Conflict

Self-Awareness Questions

As someone with an anxious attachment style, I think I experience conflict as more than just a disagreement about one issue. I also think about what it might mean for the relationship. Because of that, I pay a lot of attention to tone, body language, and whether someone seems distant. Sometimes I probably assume a person is more upset with me than they really are, which makes me more cautious. For example, in a group project, if someone answers me really briefly, I might start wondering if they are annoyed with me when they could just be stressed.

Adaptability Questions

Someone with an anxious attachment style can adapt by slowing down and separating the actual issue from the fear of losing the relationship. One good strategy is checking assumptions before reacting. Instead of assuming distance means rejection, it helps to ask calm, direct questions. It also helps to practice saying what you need without apologizing for it. If I were dealing with another anxious person, we would probably both need reassurance while still staying focused on the problem. If I were dealing with an avoidant person, I would need to give them space without taking that personally. For example, if a classmate wanted time before continuing a disagreement, I could agree to come back to it later instead of pushing for immediate reassurance.

Impact Questions

My attachment style can contribute to both escalation and de-escalation. It can help de-escalate conflict because I usually do not want to make things harsher than they need to be. I think carefully about how my words affect other people, which can help keep things respectful. But anxious attachment can also make conflict worse if I overread the situation or avoid being honest until frustration builds up. When that happens, the conflict gets bigger in my head before it even shows on the outside.

Empathy Questions

Someone with an anxious attachment style can show empathy by paying close attention to what other people are feeling while still staying grounded. I think one strength of this style is being pretty tuned in to other people’s emotions. In conflict, that can look like listening carefully, acknowledging why the other person is frustrated, and making it clear that disagreement is not the same as disrespect. For me, empathy would sound like saying, “I get why you’re frustrated, and I’m not ignoring your point.” That helps the other person feel heard without me giving up my own position.

Feedback and Improvement Questions

I have gotten feedback from my mother that I can be too passive and submissive in conflict. I get why she sees it that way, because I do tend to hold back when things are already tense. But I do not fully agree that being more forceful would automatically be better. I generally get along with more people and deal with less drama than she does, so I do not think becoming more aggressive is the answer. What I have learned from that feedback is that there is a difference between being calm and saying nothing. I do not need to become confrontational, but I do need to be more direct about what I actually need.

Cultural Sensitivity Questions

Attachment theory can be useful, but it does not apply the exact same way in every culture. Different cultures have different expectations about emotional expression, independence, family closeness, and conflict. In some places, being indirect in conflict might be seen as respectful, not insecure. In others, open emotional expression may be more normal. Because of that, attachment styles should not be treated like rigid categories that mean the same thing for everyone. Culture and family expectations both shape how these patterns show up.
